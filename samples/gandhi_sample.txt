In the early years of his political life, Gandhi developed what he called satyagraha — truth-force — as a discipline of nonviolent resistance. He believed that one could oppose injustice without hatred toward the oppressor. When the British colonial government enacted the Rowlatt Act in 1919, effectively suspending civil liberties and allowing imprisonment without trial, Gandhi organized a hartal, a general strike of shops and businesses across India. He urged workers and merchants to lay down their tools peacefully and with dignity. He was honest with his followers: the road would be long and the suffering real, but he would not ask them to endure what he was unwilling to endure himself.

He was imprisoned many times by the British authorities. He did not resist arrest. He did not call for retaliation against those who jailed him. In correspondence from Yeravda prison in 1930, he wrote that he bore no malice toward his captors and that he regarded his imprisonment as an opportunity to practice patience and inner discipline. He urged the Indian National Congress not to escalate to violence in response to his detention, even when the Salt March protests were met with police beatings at Dharasana. Reporters who witnessed the beatings described Indian volunteers walking forward in rows to be struck down without raising a hand in defense. Gandhi had prepared them for exactly this.

He was not without fault. In the early South Africa years, Gandhi made statements expressing contempt for Black Africans, drawing distinctions between Indian and African communities that reflected the prejudices of his class and time. He petitioned for separate treatment of Indians under South African law in ways that accepted rather than challenged the racial hierarchy. He later came to repudiate aspects of this period, though historians debate how completely. His record on caste was similarly complicated: he opposed untouchability and worked to integrate temples and wells, but he was slow to advocate for the abolition of the caste system itself, believing for a long time that purified caste roles could coexist with spiritual equality. His critics, including B.R. Ambedkar, argued that this position prolonged the suffering of Dalits.

He practiced what he called brahmacharya — celibacy and self-restraint — as a spiritual discipline, though the experiments he conducted with young women to test this restraint caused harm and have been rightly criticized as abusive. He justified these practices in spiritual terms, but the women involved had little power to refuse a man of his stature. His personal austerities, which he modeled publicly and expected of his ashram community, were sometimes imposed on others without their full consent.

His courage in the face of physical danger was consistent and documented. During the Noakhali riots of 1946, when Hindu-Muslim violence was spreading across Bengal, Gandhi walked alone through the countryside visiting destroyed villages, sleeping in houses of the communities most at risk, conducting prayer meetings attended by members of both faiths. His personal secretary noted that he received death threats daily during this period and refused a security escort. When partition violence reached its peak in 1947, he undertook a fast unto death in Calcutta that is credited with halting the killing in that city. He broke the fast only when leaders of both Hindu and Muslim communities presented signed pledges to protect one another's people.

He was transparent about his failures. He wrote and spoke publicly about his shame at moments of cowardice, his anger, his grief. He described the years following his son Harilal's estrangement as a period of deep personal failure, acknowledging that his commitment to his public mission had come at the cost of his duties as a father. He did not excuse himself. He did not ask his followers to ignore these failures. He asked them to hold him accountable, and to hold themselves to the same discipline of honesty about their own shortcomings.
